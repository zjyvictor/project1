•Basic Information About Snooker Table

(a) The Playing Area

According to World Professional Billiards and Snooker Association (WPBSA), the playing area of a standard table is within the cushion faces and shall measure 11 ft 8½ in x 5 ft 10 in (3569 mm × 1778 mm).


(b) Bottom Cushion and Top Cushion

The two shorter sides of the table are defined as the Bottom (also known as Baulk) and Top Cushions of the table. Where a cloth with a nap is fitted to the table, the smooth grain of the nap runs from the Bottom Cushion to the Top Cushion.


(c) Baulk-line and Baulk

A straight line drawn 29 in (737 mm) from the face of the Bottom Cushion, and parallel to it, running from side cushion to side cushion is called the Baulk-line. That line and the intervening space is termed Baulk.


(d) The “D”

The “D” is a semi-circle marked in Baulk with the centre of its straight section in the middle of the Baulk-line and with a radius of 11½ in (292 mm).


(e) Balls

A set of balls comprises of 15 Red balls, and one each of the following coloured balls: Yellow, Green, Brown, Blue, Pink, Black and a White. The White ball is the cue-ball.


(f) Spots

Marked at each corner of the “D”, viewed from the Baulk end, the one on the right is known as the Yellow Spot and the one on the left as the Green Spot. Another four spots marked on the centre longitudinal line of the table are Brown Spot, Blue Spot, Pink Spot and Black Spot.


(g) Pocket Openings

There shall be a pocket at each of the four corners of the table and one each at the middle of the longer sides.

•Snooker rules

Snooker rules are very complicated because when you play, you will encounter so many different situations. But don't worry, let me introduce very basic snooker rules and these rules can help you understand most time of a snooker game.


Players take it in turns to break (start the frame) with a coin toss deciding who starts the first frame. The break is made with the cue-ball in the D and a red must be struck.


Scoring

If either palyer grasps a chance and pots the first red successfully, which is worth 1 point, then the player can continue to play any other colored balls: Yellow (2 points), Green (3 points), Brown (4 points), Blue (5 points), Pink (6 points) and Black (7 points). After potting the colored ball, the referee will re-spot the colored ball (the reds are not re-spotted) and the player reverts to a red and alternates red, then color until all the reds are potted. The remaining six colours are then potted in ascending points order, thus finishing with the black.


Winning

The winner is the person who scores the most points in a frame. Once a player has a lead of more points than remain on the table the opponent is said to “need snookers”. A snooker is where the balls are so placed so that the player cannot directly hit the next legal ball. The hope is to force a foul and earn four points. If a player thinks they cannot win, even by forcing snookers, they concede the frame, usually when around four or more snookers (fouls) would be required in addition to all the remaining balls, depending on how many balls are left.


If you understand the rules above, you will not get confused when you see the match in most cases. If you would like to know more detailed rules of snooker, see the official rulebook publised by WPBSA.
